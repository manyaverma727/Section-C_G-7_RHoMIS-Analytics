\vspace{6pt}

\textbf{The problem.} Smallholder farming households across 35 countries face chronic food insecurity, yet the structural factors driving it — and the regions where they concentrate — remain poorly mapped at a scale useful for intervention targeting. Without clear evidence on which farm profiles carry the highest risk, development organisations and agricultural ministries cannot direct limited budgets toward the households that need them most.

\textbf{The approach.} This project analysed the Rural Household Multi-Indicator Survey (RHoMIS), a standardised dataset covering 54,873 households from 2015 to 2023. Raw survey data with 1,599 columns was cleaned and transformed through a Python ETL pipeline, then subjected to exploratory analysis, hypothesis testing, logistic regression, and k-means clustering. Findings are presented through a four-dashboard Tableau system published on Tableau Public.

\textbf{What was found.} Two in three surveyed households reported food shortage in the past year — confirming that food insecurity is the structural norm, not the exception. The largest single driver is income position within each country (odds ratio: 0.456 in a controlled model), followed by cultivated land area (0.671), land productivity (0.828), and low education (1.268 — a risk factor). Irrigation access, reaching only 20.2\% of households, creates a 16.2 percentage-point food shortage gap between irrigated and rainfed farms.

\textbf{What should be done.} Three interventions stand out: expanding irrigation in the highest-risk rainfed cluster; directing productivity support to the bottom two land productivity quartiles; and combining income diversification with basic education access for the poorest income quintile. Together these interventions address the structural roots of food insecurity rather than its symptoms.

\section{Sector \& Business Context}

Smallholder agriculture supports an estimated 500 million farm families globally and produces the majority of food consumed in developing economies. Despite this central role, smallholder households remain the most food-insecure segment of the global population — caught in a cycle where low farm productivity limits income, low income limits investment, and limited investment perpetuates low productivity.

Development organisations, international donors, and national agricultural ministries invest substantial capital in breaking this cycle. The challenge is not funding but precision: without reliable evidence on which farm profiles and which regions carry the highest risk, programmes are spread thin and their impact diluted. A field officer in Rwanda and a programme director at IFAD face the same question — where do we act first, and what do we fix? This project exists to answer that question with data.

The decision-makers this analysis serves are two: national agricultural policy teams who must allocate extension service budgets within a country, and international programme officers designing multi-country portfolio interventions. Both need a fast, visual, evidence-backed picture of vulnerability distribution — and both are served by the same four-dashboard Tableau system at the centre of this project.

The business value is straightforward. Misallocated agricultural interventions — irrigation infrastructure in low-vulnerability regions, soil support for already-productive farms — waste development capital and delay meaningful impact. A country-level vulnerability map backed by household-level statistical analysis reduces programme design time, focuses resources on high-risk populations, and strengthens the evidence base for decisions that affect millions of rural households.

\section{Problem Statement \& Objectives}

\begin{findingbox}
Which regions and farm profiles across 35 countries are most vulnerable to food insecurity and low farm income — and which combination of factors (farm size, land productivity, crop diversity, irrigation access, household demographics, and income position) drives those outcomes?
\end{findingbox}

This project treats food insecurity as a measurable, structurally-driven outcome rather than a diffuse humanitarian condition. The analysis is in scope for: food insecurity measured via the self-reported shortage flag, structural driver analysis across land, irrigation, crops, income, and education dimensions, household-level vulnerability segmentation using clustering, and country-level risk mapping. It is out of scope for: causal inference or randomised evaluation, year-over-year trend analysis (data is cross-sectional per survey wave), sub-regional administrative disaggregation (region names are not standardised across countries), and any analysis relying on the HFIAS frequency score (87.5\% missing).

Success means three things: all key findings are supported by formal statistical tests, the Tableau dashboard enables a non-technical user to navigate country and profile comparisons without assistance, and each recommendation maps to a structural factor that an intervention programme can realistically address.

\subsection{Data Limitations}

Several constraints shape what this analysis can and cannot claim. Income, land productivity, and crop harvest fields carry extreme right skew — in some cases skewness above 200 — meaning raw values cannot be plotted on continuous axes. All analysis uses median aggregation, log transformation, or within-country percentile ranking to address this. Year distribution is heavily uneven: 2018 alone accounts for 22\% of the sample while 2015 contributes only 2\%. This makes year-over-year trend analysis unreliable, so the year variable is used only as a control in regression, not for trend charting.

The HFIAS survey instrument was not consistently deployed across all waves. Many countries show 100\% food-secure classification under HFIAS — an artefact of incomplete administration, not a genuine outcome. HFIAS is used only as a validation cross-reference in this project, never as a primary metric. FIES coverage is limited to 22 of 35 countries, so any FIES-based finding is noted with its restricted population. Finally, the Philippines and Gambia have zero valid food shortage flag responses and are excluded from all food shortage rate charts.

\section{Data Cleaning \& ETL Pipeline}

\nb{notebooks/02\_cleaning.ipynb committed to GitHub}

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

All cleaning and transformation steps were executed in Python via \texttt{scripts/etl\_pipeline.py}, which serves as the canonical implementation. Notebooks 01 and 02 document and verify the same logic for transparency and auditability.

\subsection{What the Pipeline Does}

The pipeline begins by narrowing focus: 1,599 raw survey columns are reduced to 60 working columns covering the domains of identity, demographics, farm structure, crop production, income, and food security. This selection is driven by analytical relevance and missingness thresholds, not data convenience.

Country names are standardised to lowercase throughout, correcting a known misspelling of ``philippines'' that appears in a subset of records. Age fields in \texttt{age\_malehead} and \texttt{age\_femalehead} presented an unusual challenge: some survey waves recorded birth years instead of ages. The pipeline detects these cases using each row's own survey year and converts values that fall in the plausible birth-year range $(1900, \text{survey year})$ to an age. Values implying age above 110 are treated as invalid and set to missing.

Household size was a critical repair. The field \texttt{count\_people} carries 49.4\% missing values — an unacceptable gap for a variable used in nearly every downstream analysis. The fix is to sum eight age-band columns that collectively cover the full household, producing \texttt{household\_size\_derived} with zero missing values.

Land area required a unit conversion step. Survey respondents reported land in local units — ranging from hectares and acres to country-specific measures such as ``bigha'' and ``cuerda.'' The pipeline applies a 28-unit mapping table to convert each record to hectares. Units not in the mapping table produce a missing value: no guessing, no capping. This is the correct decision. The resulting maximum of 30,000 hectares in Nigeria is a verified legitimate large-farm record, not an outlier.

Binary survey fields such as \texttt{land\_irrigated} (recorded as ``y''/``n'') and \texttt{foodshortagetime} are standardised and encoded as numeric flags, which Tableau and the regression models can consume directly. Food security instrument question strings from HFIAS and FIES are retained exactly as validated strings — the value ``no\_answer'' is a legitimate survey response category, not a missing value, and is preserved as such throughout.

\subsection{What the Pipeline Does Not Do}

No imputation is applied to any field at any stage. Missing values remain as missing throughout. The missingness in RHoMIS is structurally non-random — it reflects which questions were included in which survey waves, and which households answered them. Filling missing values with means, medians, or zeros would silently misrepresent the data and produce false precision in analyses that should acknowledge their coverage constraints. Every analysis in this project reports its valid population size explicitly.

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}

\nb{notebooks/03\_eda.ipynb committed to GitHub}

\subsection{How Food Insecurity Is Distributed}

Of 47,399 households with valid food shortage responses, 67.7\% — or 32,097 households — reported experiencing a food shortage period in the past year. The median shortage lasted three months, consistent with seasonal lean-period insecurity rather than year-round deprivation. This framing matters for intervention design: food storage bridges and income smoothing programmes targeting the lean season are likely to be more cost-effective than general food assistance.

Country-level variation is extreme and cannot be explained by farm structure alone. The Democratic Republic of Congo leads among countries with at least 500 survey households at 94.5\% food shortage, followed by Rwanda (94.3\%), Senegal (91.5\%), and Comoros (90.7\%). At the other end, India records 10.5\%, Palestine 14.9\%, Nepal 18.1\%, and Costa Rica 18.6\%. This 84-point spread between the highest and lowest-risk countries confirms that national context — conflict exposure, infrastructure quality, market access, climate — is a powerful moderating factor that must be controlled when comparing structural drivers across countries.

\subsection{Irrigation: The Clearest Single Driver}

Households with irrigation access face a 55.3\% food shortage rate. Households without it face 71.5\%. That 16.2 percentage-point gap is the largest and most actionable structural difference visible in the data — and it exists against a backdrop where only 20.2\% of households have any irrigation access at all. The implication is that irrigation expansion has both a large effect size and a large addressable population. It is the intervention with the greatest potential reach.

\subsection{Land Productivity: The Deepest Structural Divide}

Splitting households into productivity quartiles reveals the most dramatic gradient in the dataset. Q1 households — those in the lowest land productivity quartile — face an 83\% food shortage rate. Q4 households face 54\%. That 29.1 percentage-point gap between the most and least productive farms is larger than the irrigation gap, larger than the income quintile gap, and — crucially — it persists after controlling for country in the regression model. Low land productivity is not a proxy for country poverty; it is an independent structural risk factor.

\subsection{Income Position}

The \texttt{country\_income\_rank\_pct} field ranks each household's income within its own country on a 0--1 scale. This design removes cross-country currency and cost-of-living differences, leaving a clean relative welfare measure with near-zero skewness (0.08). The lowest income quintile faces a food shortage rate 19.9 percentage points above the highest quintile — a consistent gradient that confirms income position as a strong predictor of vulnerability even when controlling for the farm structural variables that are partly collinear with it.

\subsection{Crop Diversity and Gender}

Households growing three or more distinct crop types show lower food shortage rates in the exploratory data. However, this effect does not survive multivariate control: crop diversity co-varies with household wealth, so much of the apparent protective effect is actually the income effect in disguise. This matters for programme design — crop diversification alone is unlikely to drive food security improvements without accompanying income support.

Gender shows a modest but statistically significant pattern. Female survey respondents report marginally higher food shortage rates in bivariate analysis (Cramér's V = 0.078). The median share of agricultural value controlled by female adults is near parity at 0.5 in the aggregate, though significant within-country heterogeneity is expected and not captured by the country-level view.

\subsection{FIES and HFIAS Validation}

Moving from 0 to 8 affirmative FIES responses corresponds to a 72.1 percentage-point increase in observed food shortage rate in the 24,097 households where both measures are available. This confirms that the self-reported food shortage question and the FIES instrument are measuring the same underlying construct. The HFIAS validation produces a similar directional result (Mann-Whitney U, $p \approx 0$), though the HFIAS instrument was not consistently administered across waves and cannot serve as a primary metric.

\section{Insights Summary}

\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{Food insecurity is the structural norm.} A 67.7\% global rate confirms that programmes designed on the assumption that most farms are food-secure will systematically miss their target population.

  \item \textbf{Irrigation offers the largest addressable gap.} The 16.2pp food shortage difference between irrigated and rainfed households, against a backdrop of only 20.2\% irrigation coverage, means that infrastructure investment reaches the most households with the largest measurable outcome change.

  \item \textbf{Low land productivity is the deepest structural risk.} The 29.1pp gap between Q1 and Q4 productivity quartiles — confirmed in the controlled regression — is an independent risk factor that is not reducible to country poverty or income differences.

  \item \textbf{Income position is the single strongest predictor.} An odds ratio of 0.456 for within-country income rank means that addressing income itself — not just its agricultural correlates — can move the food security outcome.

  \item \textbf{Low education amplifies every other risk.} With the highest Cramér's V (0.145) and an adjusted odds ratio of 1.268, low education functions as a vulnerability multiplier. Integrated programmes that pair agricultural support with literacy access should expect compounding gains.

  \item \textbf{Four countries need immediate attention.} DRC, Rwanda, Senegal, and Comoros all exceed 90\% food shortage rates in a population that can only be partially explained by farm structure. Country-level factors — conflict, infrastructure, climate — carry substantial weight and must inform programme design.

  \item \textbf{Low-risk countries are proof of concept.} India (10.5\%), Palestine (14.9\%), Nepal (18.1\%), and Costa Rica (18.6\%) show that smallholder food security can be substantially reduced. Their higher irrigation coverage and income diversification rates are worth examining as benchmarks.

  \item \textbf{The most vulnerable cluster has almost no irrigation.} The low-income rainfed cluster (14,330 households, 78.7\% shortage) has 0.3\% irrigation access — making it the clearest single target for infrastructure investment in the entire dataset.

  \item \textbf{Large farms are not protective.} The large-household extensive cluster holds significantly more land than other profiles but records a 72.3\% food shortage rate. Extensive area without productivity improvement does not translate to food security.

  \item \textbf{Crop diversity needs income support to work.} The positive EDA correlation between crop diversity and food security weakens after income controls, suggesting that diversification programmes are most effective when bundled with income access rather than delivered in isolation.

  \item \textbf{Three months of shortage implies seasonal targeting.} A 3-month median shortage duration points to the lean season as the primary trigger. Storage improvement and income-bridge programmes timed to the lean season are likely more cost-effective than general food aid.

  \item \textbf{The model enables vulnerability pre-screening.} With an ROC-AUC of 0.812 using five observable farm variables, a simplified vulnerability score derived from this model could screen households for intervention eligibility — reducing targeting cost without requiring a new full survey.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Recommendations}

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Recommendation 1: Irrigation expansion in the low-income rainfed cluster}

The low-income rainfed cluster comprises 14,330 households with a 78.7\% food shortage rate and just 0.3\% irrigation coverage. The data shows a clear, statistically confirmed 16.2 percentage-point improvement in food security outcomes for irrigated vs.\ rainfed households. Irrigation expansion — starting with small-scale and drip systems in DRC, Rwanda, Senegal, and Comoros — directly addresses the largest structural gap in the dataset. Estimated impact: approximately 2,300 households per 1,000 reached exit acute food insecurity if irrigation coverage moves from 0.3\% to 20\%. Implementation timeline: 12--36 months per programme cycle; requires water-source access audits and infrastructure capital.
\end{findingbox}

\vspace{8pt}

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Recommendation 2: Productivity support for Q1 and Q2 households}

Approximately 23,000--25,000 households in the survey population fall in the bottom two land productivity quartiles, which carry food shortage rates of 83\% and above. The logistic model confirms productivity as the strongest adjusted farm structural predictor ($p \approx 0$, OR = 0.828 per log unit). Deploying extension services — improved seed varieties, soil fertility management, and agronomic training — to Q1 and Q2 households is among the fastest and lowest-cost interventions available. Estimated impact: a 10--15pp reduction in food shortage rate for Q1 households that achieve Q2 productivity levels. Implementation timeline: within 6 months through existing extension networks.
\end{findingbox}

\vspace{8pt}

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Recommendation 3: Income diversification and education for the bottom income quintile}

Income position is the single strongest predictor of food security in the controlled model (OR = 0.456), and low education is the strongest risk amplifier (OR = 1.268). Households that combine both characteristics carry the highest cumulative vulnerability odds. The recommended intervention integrates off-farm income pathway support — rural microenterprise access, remittance facilitation — with adult literacy programmes for bottom-quintile households with low education. Estimated impact: 4--8pp food shortage reduction for households that move from the bottom to the second income quintile. Implementation: income support is deployable within 3--6 months via existing microfinance channels; education requires local government coordination.
\end{findingbox}

\section{Limitations}

The most fundamental constraint is the cross-sectional nature of the survey. Each household appears only once, which makes causal inference impossible. Every regression coefficient and every group difference reported here is an association — it describes what co-occurs with food insecurity, not what causes it. Intervention impact estimates are constructed from group comparisons, not experimental effects.

Self-reported food shortage status is subject to recall bias and social desirability effects, particularly in survey contexts where acknowledging food shortage may carry stigma. The FIES and HFIAS validation tests support the reliability of the primary measure, but objective alternatives such as dietary recall were not available in this dataset.

Income data carries extreme skew driven by multiplication artefacts in multi-crop income fields, producing values in the billions of PPP dollars. All income analysis uses the within-country income rank, which is robust to these absolute outliers and produces a near-uniform distribution (skewness: 0.08). Raw income figures are never used in any analysis or chart.

Year distribution imbalance — 2018 accounts for 22\% of the sample — rules out year-over-year trend analysis. The year variable is included only as a fixed effect in regression, not as a meaningful analytical dimension. Sub-national region names are not standardised across countries, preventing any consistent sub-national comparison. Finally, the HFIAS frequency score is 87.5\% missing and was not used in any primary analysis.

% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Future Scope}

The most impactful extension would be longitudinal panel linkage. If the same households could be traced across survey years, within-household change estimates would move the analysis from association toward causal inference — enabling programme impact evaluation rather than mere profiling.

The logistic regression model (ROC-AUC 0.812) is already strong enough to serve as a lightweight household vulnerability scoring tool. Applied to new survey waves as they are collected, a simplified version of the model could pre-screen households for intervention eligibility without requiring a full new analytical cycle, significantly reducing programme targeting costs.

On the data side, linking household GPS coordinates to satellite-derived rainfall, NDVI (vegetation index), and temperature anomaly data would allow weather-year context to be separated from structural farm characteristics in the model — isolating the long-run structural drivers from year-specific climate shocks. Standardisation of sub-national region names across survey waves would unlock within-country geographic disaggregation down to district or province level. Finally, a live-connected Tableau dashboard pulling from an updating survey pipeline would allow development organisations to monitor programme-area trends as new waves arrive, rather than waiting for a periodic full refresh.

\section{Conclusion}

This project applied a full data analytics pipeline to a single urgent question: which smallholder farming households, across 35 countries, face the highest food insecurity risk, and what drives it? The analysis of 54,873 households confirmed that food insecurity is pervasive at 67.7\%, geographically concentrated in Central and West Africa, and structurally driven by a small number of addressable factors. Income position (odds ratio: 0.456), cultivated land area (0.671), land productivity (0.828), and low education (1.268) emerge from a country-controlled logistic model with an ROC-AUC of 0.812 — strong enough to support household-level vulnerability targeting. The clearest single action is irrigation expansion: reaching the 79.8\% of households currently without it, beginning with the low-income rainfed cluster at 78.7\% food shortage and 0.3\% irrigation coverage, is associated with a 16 percentage-point improvement in food security probability. Development organisations that concentrate irrigation, productivity support, and income diversification on the highest-risk cluster will reach the largest food-insecure population with the highest-return combination of evidence-based interventions.
