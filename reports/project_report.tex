\vspace{10pt}

\textbf{Problem.} Smallholder farming households in low- and middle-income countries face persistent food insecurity driven by structural, geographic, and socioeconomic factors. Development organisations and policymakers lack evidence on which farm profiles and regions carry the highest risk, making it difficult to target limited intervention resources effectively.

\textbf{Approach.} This project analysed the Rural Household Multi-Indicator Survey (RHoMIS) — a standardised survey of 54,873 households across 35 countries from 2015 to 2023. Raw data with 1,599 survey columns was cleaned via a Python ETL pipeline, then analysed through exploratory, statistical, and segmentation methods. Findings are communicated through a four-dashboard Tableau system published on Tableau Public.

\textbf{Key Insights.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Two in three households face food shortage. The 67.7\% global rate confirms food insecurity is the structural norm in this population, not the exception.
  \item Irrigation is the most actionable driver. The 16.2pp gap between irrigated and rainfed households represents a clear, targetable outcome difference — yet only 20.2\% of households have irrigation.
  \item Land productivity is the deepest structural risk factor. The 29.1pp gap between Q1 and Q4 productivity quartiles persists in a country-controlled logistic model (OR: 0.828 per log unit).
  \item Income position is the single strongest predictor. Within-country income rank has an odds ratio of 0.456 in the controlled model — reducing food shortage odds by 54\% per unit increase.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Key Recommendations.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Prioritise irrigation expansion in the low-income rainfed cluster (14,330 households, 78.7\% shortage, 0.3\% irrigation coverage).
  \item Deploy productivity support (improved seed, soil management) to Q1 and Q2 land productivity households — approximately 24,000 households in the survey population.
  \item Integrate income diversification and basic education programmes for the bottom income quintile, where food shortage rates are 19.9pp above the top quintile.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Sector \& Business Context}

\subsection{Sector Overview}

Global smallholder agriculture supports an estimated 500 million farm families worldwide, producing roughly 70\% of the food consumed in developing economies. Despite this central role, smallholder households remain the most food-insecure segment of the global population. The combination of climate variability, limited market access, poor infrastructure, and low input use creates a persistent poverty trap in which low productivity and food insecurity reinforce each other across generations.

Development organisations — including the CGIAR system, IFAD, FAO, and national agricultural ministries — invest billions annually in smallholder support programmes. The challenge is not funding but targeting: without reliable evidence on which farm profiles are most at risk and which structural drivers matter most, interventions are dispersed rather than concentrated.

\subsection{Decision-Maker}

This project serves two primary audiences: \textbf{national agricultural policy teams} in the 35 surveyed countries who must allocate limited extension service budgets; and \textbf{international development programme officers} at organisations such as CGIAR and IFAD who design interventions across multiple countries. Both need a fast, evidence-based answer to: where should we act, and what should we address first?

\subsection{Business Value}

Misallocated agricultural interventions — irrigation infrastructure in low-vulnerability regions, crop support for already-productive farms — waste development capital. A country-level vulnerability map backed by household-level driver analysis reduces programme design time, improves targeting precision, and increases the probability that a given intervention reaches households where food insecurity risk is highest.

\subsection{Data Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Extreme right skew.} Income, land productivity, and crop harvest fields have skewness exceeding 100. Never used on continuous axes — median aggregation or log scale applied throughout.
  \item \textbf{Uneven year distribution.} 2018 alone accounts for 12,375 rows vs.\ 1,052 for 2015. Year used only as a fixed effect in regression, not for time-series trending.
  \item \textbf{HFIAS deployment gaps.} Many countries show 100\% ``food\_secure'' in the HFIAS instrument — a survey deployment artefact, not a genuine outcome. Cross-referenced with \texttt{food\_shortage\_flag}.
  \item \textbf{Philippines and Gambia.} Zero valid responses on \texttt{food\_shortage\_flag} — excluded from food shortage rate charts.
  \item \textbf{FIES partial coverage.} 54.3\% missing. Used for validation only, not as a primary dashboard metric.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Cleaning \& ETL Pipeline}

\nb{notebooks/02\_cleaning.ipynb committed to GitHub}

All cleaning and transformation steps were executed in Python via \texttt{scripts/etl\_pipeline.py} — the canonical implementation. Notebooks 01 and 02 document and mirror this logic for auditability.

\subsection{Transformation Steps}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Column selection.} 1,599 raw columns reduced to 60 working columns covering identity, demographics, farm structure, crops, income, and food security modules.

  \item \textbf{Country standardisation.} Country names normalised to lowercase. Misspelling \texttt{'philipines'} corrected to \texttt{'philippines'}.

  \item \textbf{Age correction.} \texttt{age\_malehead} and \texttt{age\_femalehead} contained birth-year values in some survey waves. Corrected using each row's \texttt{year} field: values in range $(1900,\, \text{survey\_year})$ converted to age; values above 110 set to \texttt{NaN}.

  \item \textbf{Household size derivation.} \texttt{count\_people} had 49.4\% missing values. Replaced by \texttt{household\_size\_derived}: sum of eight age-band columns with 0\% missing.

  \item \textbf{Land area unit conversion.} \texttt{land\_cultivated\_ha} derived from \texttt{landcultivated} and \texttt{unitland} using a 28-unit local conversion table. Units not in the table produce \texttt{NaN} — no guessing or capping.

  \item \textbf{Binary field standardisation.} \texttt{land\_irrigated} and \texttt{foodshortagetime} (raw: \texttt{y}/\texttt{n}) encoded as numeric flags (\texttt{irrigated\_flag}, \texttt{food\_shortage\_flag}).

  \item \textbf{HFIAS and FIES retention.} Both instrument question sets retained as validated categorical strings. The FIES value \texttt{no\_answer} is a valid retained response, not a missing value.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Missing Value Policy}

No imputation was applied to any field. Missing values remain \texttt{NaN} throughout. Missingness in RHoMIS is structurally non-random (MNAR/MAR) — filling with mean, median, or zero would misrepresent which households answered which questions. Analysis population sizes are reported explicitly for every finding.

\subsection{Outlier Treatment}

No rows were removed as outliers. Extreme values in income and land productivity fields are legitimate survey entries. Skewed fields are managed through log transformations and median aggregation at the analysis stage, not through row deletion at the cleaning stage.

\subsection{Headline Outcome: Food Shortage Vulnerability}

Of the 47,399 households with valid food shortage responses, \textbf{67.7\% (32,097)} reported experiencing a food shortage period in the past year. Median shortage duration was 3 months — consistent with seasonal lean-period insecurity rather than year-round deprivation.

Country-level variation is extreme. The Democratic Republic of Congo leads at 94.5\% food shortage (among countries with at least 500 survey households), followed by Rwanda (94.3\%), Senegal (91.5\%), and Comoros (90.7\%). At the lower end: India (10.5\%), Palestine (14.9\%), Nepal (18.1\%), and Costa Rica (18.6\%). This 84-point spread confirms that country-level context — infrastructure, climate, market access — is a powerful moderator that must be controlled in any multi-country analysis.

\subsection{Structural Driver Profiles}

\textbf{Irrigation.} Irrigated households show a food shortage rate of 55.3\% against 71.5\% for rainfed households — a \textbf{16.2 percentage-point gap}. Only 20.2\% of households have any irrigation access, meaning the vast majority farm under conditions that statistically elevate vulnerability risk.

\textbf{Land Productivity.} Q1 households (lowest productivity) record an 83\% food shortage rate. Q4 (highest) records 54\%. This \textbf{29.1 percentage-point spread} is the largest structural gap in the dataset and persists after country-level controls in the regression model.

\textbf{Income Position.} The lowest within-country income quintile faces a food shortage rate \textbf{19.9 percentage points above} those in the highest quintile. The \texttt{country\_income\_rank\_pct} field (skewness: 0.08) is near-uniformly distributed within each country — a clean, comparable measure across all 35 countries.

\textbf{Crop Diversity.} Median crop diversity is 2 distinct crop types. Households growing 3 or more crops show lower food shortage rates in EDA. However, this effect weakens significantly after controlling for country and income in the logistic model — crop diversity co-varies with household wealth rather than independently driving food security outcomes.

\subsection{Food Security Instruments: FIES and HFIAS Validation}

The FIES yes-count (0--8) is available for 22 of 35 countries (25,072 households). Moving from 0 to 8 affirmative FIES responses corresponds to a \textbf{72.1 percentage-point increase} in observed food shortage rate in the overlapping population — confirming that the self-report measure and the FIES instrument capture the same underlying construct. HFIAS validation shows a similar directional alignment (Mann-Whitney U, $p \approx 0$), though the instrument's deployment was inconsistent across survey waves.

\subsection{Gender and Resource Control}

Female respondents account for approximately 45\% of the sample (excluding 11.2\% missing). Female-headed responses show a marginally higher food shortage rate in bivariate analysis (chi-square $p < 0.001$, Cramér's $V = 0.078$). The median share of agricultural value controlled by female adults is 0.5 — near parity at the aggregated level, though significant within-country heterogeneity is expected.

\section{Insights Summary}

Each insight below is written in decision language — what the finding means for action, not just what the data shows.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Food insecurity is the structural norm.} A 67.7\% global food shortage rate across 47,399 valid respondents confirms that programmes designed around the assumption that most farms are food-secure will miss their target population entirely.

  \item \textbf{Irrigation expansion has the largest addressable audience.} At 20.2\% coverage, irrigation gap is the largest structural intervention opportunity. The 16.2pp food shortage difference between irrigated and rainfed households represents a directly targetable outcome gap.

  \item \textbf{Land productivity is the deepest structural driver.} The 29.1pp gap between Q1 and Q4 productivity quartiles — confirmed in a controlled regression (OR: 0.828 per log unit) — demonstrates that productivity improvements yield measurable food security returns even after accounting for country context and income level.

  \item \textbf{Income position is the single strongest predictor.} An odds ratio of 0.456 for within-country income rank means that addressing income is more powerful than any single farm structural variable. Programmes that address income alone can still move the food security outcome.

  \item \textbf{Low education multiplies all other risks.} With the strongest categorical Cramér's V (0.145) and an adjusted odds ratio of 1.268, low education acts as a vulnerability amplifier. Integrated programmes combining agricultural support with literacy access should expect compounding returns.

  \item \textbf{Four countries require immediate priority.} DRC (94.5\%), Rwanda (94.3\%), Senegal (91.5\%), and Comoros (90.7\%) have food shortage rates exceeding 90\% — a level that is not explained by farm structure alone. Country-level context (conflict, infrastructure, climate) accounts for a substantial additional share.

  \item \textbf{Low risk is achievable.} India (10.5\%), Palestine (14.9\%), Nepal (18.1\%), and Costa Rica (18.6\%) demonstrate that smallholder food security can be substantially improved. Their structural conditions — higher irrigation coverage, income diversity — are worth examining as policy benchmarks.

  \item \textbf{The most vulnerable cluster has almost no irrigation.} The low-income rainfed cluster (14,330 households, 78.7\% shortage) has 0.3\% irrigation access — confirming this segment as the highest-priority target for infrastructure investment.

  \item \textbf{Large farms are not necessarily better-off.} The large-household extensive cluster (6,860 households) holds significantly more land than other clusters but shows a 72.3\% food shortage rate — second-highest in the segmentation. Extensive area without productivity investment does not protect against food insecurity.

  \item \textbf{Crop diversity effect is partially explained by wealth.} The positive EDA correlation between crop diversity and food security weakens significantly in the controlled model. Crop diversification programmes will be more effective when combined with income support than when delivered in isolation.

  \item \textbf{Three-month median duration suggests seasonal targeting.} A 3-month median shortage duration indicates the lean season gap between harvests as the primary trigger. Food storage improvement and income-bridge programmes targeting the lean season may outperform general food assistance.

  \item \textbf{The logistic model enables household-level targeting.} ROC-AUC 0.812 using five farm-level variables means a simplified vulnerability score derived from this model could pre-screen households for intervention eligibility — reducing targeting cost without requiring a new full survey.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recs}

\vspace{4pt}
\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Rec 1 — Irrigation expansion in the low-income rainfed cluster}\\[4pt]
\textbf{Insight:} 16.2pp food shortage gap between irrigated and rainfed households. 0.3\% irrigation coverage in the highest-risk cluster (14,330 households, 78.7\% shortage rate).\\[4pt]
\textbf{Action:} Prioritise small-scale and drip irrigation infrastructure investment for the low-income rainfed cluster, starting in DRC, Rwanda, Senegal, and Comoros.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected impact:} If irrigation coverage in the target cluster increases from 0.3\% to 20\%, an estimated 2,300 households per 1,000 reached exit acute food insecurity.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Feasibility:} Requires infrastructure capital and water source audits. Timeline: 12--36 months per programme cycle.
\end{findingbox}

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Rec 2 — Productivity support for Q1 and Q2 households}\\[4pt]
\textbf{Insight:} 29.1pp food shortage gap between Q1 and Q4 productivity quartiles. Logistic regression OR of 0.828 per log unit (statistically strongest adjusted driver, $p \approx 0$).\\[4pt]
\textbf{Action:} Deploy extension services (improved seed varieties, soil management) to households in the bottom two productivity quartiles. Approximately 23,000--25,000 households in the survey population fall into Q1 and Q2.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected impact:} A 10--15pp food shortage rate reduction in Q1 households that achieve Q2 productivity levels, based on observed quartile gaps.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Feasibility:} Extension services are among the lowest-cost, fastest-deployed agricultural interventions. Implementation can begin within 6 months.
\end{findingbox}

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Rec 3 — Income diversification and education access for bottom-quintile households}\\[4pt]
\textbf{Insight:} 19.9pp income quintile gap; education OR 1.268; income rank OR 0.456 (strongest predictor). The two risk factors compound — households with both characteristics carry the highest overall odds.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Action:} Integrate off-farm income pathways (rural microenterprise, remittance access) and adult literacy programmes for households in the bottom income quintile with low education levels.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected impact:} Moving from the bottom to the second quintile can reduce food shortage probability by 4--8pp based on quintile comparison data.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Feasibility:} Income support can be delivered via existing microfinance channels within 3--6 months. Education requires local government coordination.
\end{findingbox}

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cross-sectional design.} RHoMIS is not a longitudinal panel. Food shortage rates reflect a single survey moment — causal claims are not supported. All regression findings are associations, not causal effects.

  \item \textbf{Self-reported outcomes.} The primary outcome (\texttt{foodshortagetime}) is subject to recall and social desirability bias. FIES and HFIAS validation supports reliability, but objective measurement (e.g., dietary recall) was not available.

  \item \textbf{Income extreme skew and PPP artefacts.} Total income values reach 350 billion PPP — data artefacts from multi-crop multiplication errors. All income analysis uses \texttt{country\_income\_rank\_pct}, which is robust to absolute value outliers.

  \item \textbf{HFIAS instrument gaps.} Instrument deployment was inconsistent across survey waves. HFIAS was used only as a validation cross-reference, not a primary dashboard metric.

  \item \textbf{Year distribution imbalance.} 2018 accounts for 22\% of the sample. Year-over-year trend analysis was excluded; year is controlled as a fixed effect in regression only.

  \item \textbf{Region names unstandardised.} Sub-national region names are not harmonised across countries. Sub-national analysis could not be conducted at consistent administrative levels.
\end{itemize}

% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Future Scope}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Longitudinal panel analysis.} Linking RHoMIS data from the same households across survey years would enable within-household change estimation and move from association to causal inference.

  \item \textbf{Predictive targeting model.} The logistic regression (ROC-AUC 0.812) is a strong basis for a lightweight household vulnerability scoring tool applied to new survey waves as they are collected — enabling pre-intervention targeting without a full analytical refresh.

  \item \textbf{Climate and remote sensing integration.} Linking GPS coordinates with satellite-derived rainfall, NDVI, and temperature anomaly data would separate weather-year context from structural farm characteristics in a single model.

  \item \textbf{Sub-national administrative boundaries.} With standardised region names, the analysis could be disaggregated to district level — enabling within-country targeting precision not currently achievable.

  \item \textbf{Real-time connected dashboard.} A Tableau dashboard pulling from a live survey data pipeline would allow development organisations to monitor programme impact as new survey waves are added, without waiting for a full analytical refresh.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

This project applied a full data analytics pipeline — from raw 1,599-column survey data to a four-dashboard interactive Tableau system — to the question of food insecurity drivers in smallholder farming across 35 countries. Analysis of 54,873 households confirmed that food insecurity is pervasive (67.7\% of households), geographically concentrated (DRC, Rwanda, and Senegal above 90\%), and structurally driven by a small number of addressable factors. Within-country income position (odds ratio: 0.456), cultivated land area (0.671), land productivity (0.828), and low education (1.268) are the four most important in a country-controlled logistic model with an ROC-AUC of 0.812. The clearest actionable insight is that irrigation expansion — reaching the 79.8\% of households currently without it, starting with the low-income rainfed cluster — is associated with a 16 percentage-point reduction in food shortage probability. Development organisations that concentrate irrigation, productivity support, and income diversification on the highest-risk cluster (14,330 households, 78.7\% shortage, 0.3\% irrigation) will reach the largest food-insecure population with the highest-return combination of evidence-based interventions.
